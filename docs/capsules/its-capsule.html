Before and after, on a trend

An intervention lands mid-series. Segmented regression separates two effects: the immediate jump in level, and the change in slope thereafter — each measured against the trend the series was already on.

Causal · ITS — living · live re-run illustrative demo data

What this means — in plain language

This measures the effect of an intervention by looking at the trend before it started and asking whether the line suddenly jumped, bent, or both, at that moment.

Protocol

Model

SeriesEqually-spaced observations over time, with an intervention at a known period. RegressionYₜ = β₀ + β₁·time + β₂·after + β₃·(time since intervention). Estimandsβ₂ = immediate level change; β₃ = change in slope after the intervention. CounterfactualThe pre-intervention trend extended forward.

03

Assumptions

ITS assumes the pre-intervention trend would have continued unchanged, no co-intervention coincided with the change point, and the outcome isn't driven by other time-varying factors. Autocorrelation inflates significance — production models (Prais–Winsten, ARIMA) correct for it; this capsule uses OLS and flags the caveat.

06

Interpretation

—

A negative β₂ with a near-zero β₃ is a one-off level drop; a near-zero β₂ with negative β₃ is a gradual divergence; both negative is an immediate drop that keeps widening. Read β₂ and β₃ together against the dashed counterfactual. OLS standard errors ignore autocorrelation — treat the p-values as optimistic.

How to read this · limitations. An interrupted time series attributes a change in level or slope at the intervention point to the intervention; it assumes the pre-intervention trend would have continued and is vulnerable to other events at the same time and to autocorrelation. A control series strengthens it. Demo data are illustrative.
