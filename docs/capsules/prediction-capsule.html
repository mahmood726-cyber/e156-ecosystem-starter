Does it rank, and does it match?

A risk model must do two things: discriminate (rank those who have the event above those who don't — the AUC) and calibrate (its predicted risks match observed frequencies). A high AUC with poor calibration still misleads patients.

Primary · TRIPOD — living · live re-run illustrative demo data

What this means — in plain language

A risk model has to do two things: rank patients correctly (discrimination) and get the actual probabilities right (calibration). A high AUC with poor calibration still misleads — this shows both at once.

Self-audit — internal-consistency checks

Every check runs live against the current data. The capsule must agree with itself before asking the world to agree with it.

—
How to read this · limitations. Discrimination (AUC) and calibration are different things — a model can rank well yet be systematically over- or under-confident. These are apparent-performance metrics on the data shown; real validation needs an external or held-out sample. Demo data are illustrative.

03

Measures

Discrimination ≠ calibration. A model can rank perfectly (AUC = 1) yet be miscalibrated (e.g. all predictions doubled). Both are reported; calibration is the one most often neglected and most consequential for individual decisions.

06

Interpretation

—

Calibration slope < 1 indicates over-fitting (extreme predictions too extreme); the intercept (calibration-in-the-large) shifts all predictions if non-zero. AUC ≈ 0.5 is chance, 0.7–0.8 acceptable, >0.8 strong. These are apparent (in-sample) metrics — optimism-corrected or externally-validated performance is what TRIPOD ultimately requires.
