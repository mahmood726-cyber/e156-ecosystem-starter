Are the missing studies missing for a reason?

Small studies that found nothing are the ones that quietly never get published — so the surviving literature tilts. This capsule looks for that tilt four ways: the funnel's shape, Egger's regression of the tilt, the studies trim-and-fill thinks are absent, and the effect PET-PEESE projects you'd see with no small-study effect at all.

What this means — in plain language

Small studies that found nothing often never get published, tilting the surviving literature. This looks for that tilt four different ways and estimates what the effect would be without it.

01

The question behind the funnel

small-study effects

What asymmetry would mean

EffectA standardised mean difference (SMD / Hedges' g) per study — positive favours treatment. PrecisionEach study's standard error; small studies sit low in the funnel, large studies near the top. SymmetryIf results are unbiased, studies scatter symmetrically around the pooled effect inside the funnel. AsymmetryAn empty corner — small studies only on one side — is the fingerprint of selective publication (or true small-study effects).

Tests of funnel asymmetry are low-powered for k < 10 and cannot distinguish publication bias from genuine small-study effects. Trim-and-fill is a sensitivity analysis, never the primary result.

04

Egger's regression test

radial / SND form

—

Egger regresses the standard normal deviate (effect/SE) on precision (1/SE); a non-zero intercept is the asymmetry signal, tested on k−2 df with the t-distribution. For binary outcomes Egger's test over-rejects — use Peters' or Harbord's test instead (shown in the R script).

05

Duval–Tweedie trim-and-fill

L0 estimator

—

Iteratively trims the over-represented side, re-centres, and estimates the number of suppressed studies (L0 estimator); the trimmed studies are then mirrored back and the effect re-pooled. This is a sensitivity analysis — the imputed studies are not real. A large shift between the observed and adjusted estimate is the finding, not the adjusted value itself.

06

PET-PEESE

conditional estimator

—

PET regresses effect on SE (precision-weighted); its intercept estimates the effect at SE = 0. PET first: if its intercept is significant (genuine effect beyond small-study effects), switch to PEESE (regression on SE²) for the corrected point estimate; otherwise report PET. The conditional order matters — reporting PEESE unconditionally biases away from null.

How to read this · limitations. Funnel-plot tests detect small-study effects, which may be publication bias or genuine heterogeneity; Egger's test uses the radial t-distribution and has low power below ~10 studies, and trim-and-fill is a sensitivity analysis, never the primary result.
