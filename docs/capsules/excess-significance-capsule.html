Too many wins to be real?

If a literature reports more significant studies than the studies' own power could plausibly produce, something is selecting the positives. The excess-significance test counts observed wins against the wins their power predicts.

Meta-research · TES — living · live re-run illustrative demo data

What this means — in plain language

If a set of small studies reports far more positive results than their statistical power could plausibly produce, that's a fingerprint of bias. This compares the significant studies you'd expect with the number actually seen.

01

Protocol

Question

InputStudies of one association, each as an effect estimate with a 95% CI. OObserved number of statistically significant studies (p < .05). EExpected number = Σ of each study's power to detect an assumed true effect. Testχ² comparing O and E (1 df). O ≫ E signals excess significance / selective reporting.
02

Search

Estimates are illustrative and editable in stage 4. Choose the assumed true effect (the test is sensitive to it) — fixed-effect pooled or the largest study.

03

Screening

One effect estimate per study, on the log scale (OR/RR/HR). Power is computed against the chosen plausible effect.

04

Studies — editable

OR + 95% CI

Each study contributes an OR with its 95% CI (→ log-OR and SE). A study is "significant" if its CI excludes 1. Power is each study's chance of significance if the true effect equals the assumed effect below.

06

Interpretation

—

Excess significance (O significantly > E) is a marker of reporting/selection bias, not proof of it, and the test is sensitive to the assumed effect — that is why both the fixed-effect and largest-study choices are offered. It has low power with few studies, so a non-significant test does not clear a literature.

How to read this · limitations. The test for excess significance compares how many studies were significant against how many you would expect given their power; it needs a defensible effect-size assumption (power is computed under it) and a useful number of studies. Demo data are illustrative.
