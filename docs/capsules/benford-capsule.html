Do the leading digits look natural?

Across many natural datasets the leading digit is a 1 about 30% of the time, a 9 under 5%. Reported numbers that depart sharply from Benford's law can flag rounding, fabrication, or a process that isn't what it seems — a cheap first screen, never a verdict.

Meta-research · Benford — living · live re-run illustrative demo data

What this means — in plain language

Genuine, unmanipulated numbers follow a predictable pattern in their leading digit. This checks a set of reported figures against that pattern — a large mismatch is a prompt to look closer, not proof of anything.

Protocol

Question

InputA list of reported numbers spanning several orders of magnitude (sample sizes, counts, totals). LawP(first digit = d) = log₁₀(1 + 1/d), d = 1…9. TestsPearson χ² goodness-of-fit (8 df) and Nigrini's mean absolute deviation (MAD). CaveatBenford applies to scale-spanning natural data, not bounded or assigned numbers; non-conformity is a flag, not proof.

06

Interpretation

—

Nigrini's first-digit MAD thresholds: <0.006 close conformity, 0.006–0.012 acceptable, 0.012–0.015 marginal, >0.015 nonconformity. χ² is sensitive to sample size (significant for large n even when deviations are tiny), so MAD is reported alongside. Benford screening flags datasets for review — it never establishes misconduct.
