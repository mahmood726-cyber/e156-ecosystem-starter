Is the model worth acting on?

Discrimination and calibration tell you whether a model is right; they don't tell you whether using it beats the two trivial policies — treat everyone, or treat no one. Decision curve analysis weighs true against false positives at the threshold a clinician would actually act on, and asks: across the plausible thresholds, does the model earn its keep?

What this means — in plain language

A risk model is only useful if acting on it beats the two simple policies — treat everyone, or treat no one. Across the thresholds a clinician might actually use, this shows whether the model earns its keep.

What net benefit means

Threshold ptThe risk at which a patient (or clinician) would opt for treatment — it encodes how many false positives are worth one true positive: the odds pt/(1−pt). Net benefitNB = TP/N − (FP/N)·pt/(1−pt) — true positives credited, false positives penalised at the threshold's exchange rate. Treat allIntervene on everyone; net benefit falls as the threshold rises. Treat noneNet benefit is exactly zero everywhere — the floor any useful model must beat.

A model is clinically useful over the range of thresholds where its curve sits above both treat-all and treat-none. Net benefit is on the scale of true positives per patient; multiply by 100 for "per 100 patients".
