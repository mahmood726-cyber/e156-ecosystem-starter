How strong is the association, on average?

Correlations can't be averaged directly — near ±1 their sampling distribution is badly skewed, so a plain mean is biased. Fisher's z transform straightens it out: pool on the z scale, where the variance is a clean 1/(n−3), then bend the answer back to a correlation with a prediction interval for the next study.

What this means — in plain language

Correlations can't simply be averaged. This pools them properly and reports the average strength of the association, plus where the next study's correlation would most likely fall.

Why not just average the r's

The transform

Per studyA Pearson correlation r and the sample size n it was computed from. z = atanh(r)Fisher's variance-stabilising transform; its sampling variance is exactly 1/(n−3) — independent of the true correlation. PoolInverse-variance random-effects pooling on the z scale (DerSimonian–Laird). ReportBack-transform the pooled z and its interval to r; the prediction interval says where the next study's correlation should land.

Averaging raw correlations biases the estimate toward zero and breaks the symmetry of the confidence interval. The variance is 1/(n−3), not 1/(n−2); r is clamped to ±0.9999 so z stays finite.
