How much is it worth knowing for sure?

A cost-effectiveness decision can be right on average yet wrong often — and acting on the wrong option costs health. The expected value of perfect information is the average loss from that uncertainty: the most a perfectly informative study could ever be worth. Where it's large, more research pays; where it's near zero, decide and move on.

What this means — in plain language

A decision can be right on average yet wrong often, and acting on the wrong option costs health. This puts a price on that uncertainty — the most a perfectly informative study could ever be worth — to show whether more research pays.

What EVPI measures

Net benefitFor each strategy and PSA draw: NMB = λ·QALYs − Cost, where λ is willingness-to-pay per QALY. Current infoChoose the strategy with the highest mean net benefit — max of the averages. Perfect infoIf you knew each draw's truth, you'd pick that draw's best — the average of the maxima. EVPIPerfect minus current = E[max] − max[E] ≥ 0: the expected opportunity loss from deciding under uncertainty.

EVPI is highest where strategies are close (the decision could easily flip) and falls to zero where one option clearly wins. Population EVPI scales it by everyone the decision affects — the ceiling on what further research could be worth.

How to read this · limitations. Expected value of perfect information is the per-person value of removing all decision uncertainty — an upper bound on what any future study could be worth; it is computed from the PSA, so it inherits every assumption baked into those distributions. Compare population EVPI against realistic study costs. Demo data are illustrative.
