An association, net of what we measured

Observational effects are confounded until adjusted — and never fully, because some confounders go unmeasured. This capsule shows the crude-to-adjusted shift and the E-value: how strong an unmeasured confounder would have to be to explain the result away.

What this means — in plain language

Observational findings can be confounded. This shows the crude versus adjusted effect, a map of the assumed causes, and how strong an unmeasured confounder would have to be to explain the whole result away.

The E-value (VanderWeele & Ding) is the minimum association — on the risk-ratio scale — that an unmeasured confounder would need with both the exposure and the outcome, above and beyond the measured covariates, to fully explain the observed effect. A large E-value relative to plausible confounders means the finding is robust; a small one means a modest unmeasured confounder could erase it.

How to read this · limitations. An observational association is not a causal effect: adjustment can only remove confounding by the variables you measured and list, and residual or unmeasured confounding remains. Compare the crude and adjusted estimates and state every covariate. Demo data are illustrative.
