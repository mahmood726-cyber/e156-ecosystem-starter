Is the extra health worth the extra cost?

An intervention that costs more and helps more is worth it only below a threshold price per unit of health. The ICER is that price; the net monetary benefit turns it into a single accept/reject number against a willingness-to-pay.

HTA · ICER — living · live re-run illustrative demo data

What this means — in plain language

A treatment that costs more and helps more is only worth it below a price you're willing to pay for a unit of health. This shows that trade-off and turns it into a single accept-or-reject answer at your chosen threshold.

Self-audit — internal-consistency checks

Every check runs live against the current data. The capsule must agree with itself before asking the world to agree with it.

—
How to read this · limitations. The cost-effectiveness plane and ICER summarise a single point estimate; an ICER is ambiguous in the south-east / north-west quadrants and hides decision uncertainty — the net-benefit framing (INMB at a willingness-to-pay λ) is less error-prone. Demo data are illustrative.

Perspective

Costs and QALYs should be discounted, lifetime (or a stated horizon), and from a consistent perspective (e.g. healthcare payer). This capsule takes the totals as given; garbage-in still gives a tidy ICER, so the model behind these numbers matters more than the arithmetic here.

06

Decision

—

An ICER is only interpretable in the north-east quadrant (more effective, more costly). A strategy that is cheaper and more effective dominates (always adopt); one that is costlier and less effective is dominated (never adopt) — the ICER's sign is then meaningless. The INMB sidesteps this: positive = adopt at this λ. This is the deterministic (point-estimate) result; uncertainty needs probabilistic sensitivity analysis (the CEAC capsule).
